## 1. Executive Summary

OMRAN ERP is an AI-assisted construction and renovation management system. It helps a manager move from the first project request to an organized proposal, workshop planning, execution tracking, project monitoring, and invoicing.

The product is designed for a construction or renovation company that manages customers, project sites, external workshops, tasks, issues, materials, photos, progress, and billing in one place.

The main business goal is simple: reduce scattered manual work and give managers a clearer, faster, and more controlled way to handle construction projects.

OMRAN ERP combines classic project management with AI support. The AI helps collect project information, ask follow-up questions, structure proposals, summarize project health, and recommend next actions. The manager remains responsible for reviewing, correcting, and approving final decisions.

### Core value in one sentence

OMRAN ERP turns informal construction project conversations into structured project records, clear proposals, coordinated workshop plans, tracked execution, and better management visibility.

### Current product maturity

The current system is a functional integrated prototype. It is suitable for demonstrations, evaluation, internal testing, and controlled pilot usage. It is not yet positioned as a fully production-ready enterprise product without further security, access control, operational hardening, and large-scale performance validation.

## 3. Product Vision

The product vision is:

**From project request to controlled execution in one management workspace.**

This means the system is not only a place to store customers or invoices. It is intended to support the full construction business journey:

1. A customer describes a renovation need.
2. The manager captures the request through text or voice.
3. AI helps organize the conversation into usable project information.
4. A proposal is created and reviewed.
5. Work areas are defined.
6. Workshops are assigned.

The system is built around the idea that construction projects are messy in real life. Clients may describe needs informally. Project details may arrive in pieces. Work may happen across multiple rooms or sites. Different workshops may be needed for different trades. OMRAN ERP creates structure around that complexity.

## 5. The Business Problem

Construction and renovation companies often manage work through a mixture of phone calls, handwritten notes, messages, photos, spreadsheets, and separate billing documents. This creates several common problems.

### 5.1 Scattered project information

Project details may be spread across chats, emails, calls, notebooks, and individual employee memory. This makes it hard to know which details are official.

### 5.2 Incomplete project intake

Customers may provide partial information. A manager may know the client wants a bathroom renovation, but not the exact scope, required trades, start date, materials, payment expectations, or workshop needs.

### 5.3 Slow proposal preparation

Preparing a proposal manually can take time because information must be rewritten, organized, formatted, and checked.

### 5.4 Confusing work area planning

A construction project is rarely one single task. It may include a kitchen, bathroom, facade, hallway, roof, or several apartments. Each area can require different trades and different timing.

### 5.5 Workshop scheduling issues

External workshops or subcontractors may overlap, conflict, or be assigned without clear availability. This can delay projects and create confusion.

### 5.6 Weak progress visibility

Managers may only discover delays after they become serious. Without structured progress, task, issue, material, and photo tracking, it is difficult to know whether a project is healthy.

### 5.7 Missing accountability

If issues, blockers, and updates are not recorded, it becomes hard to understand what happened, who was responsible, and what action is needed.

### 5.8 Manual billing work

Invoices and related documents often require repeated copying of project, customer, and work information.

### 5.9 Language barriers

Construction teams and customers may communicate in different languages. A project system should support Arabic, German, and English rather than forcing one language for all users.

## 9. Complete Business Workflow

This section explains the full intended project journey from first request to business follow-up.

### 9.1 New project request

A customer contacts the company with a construction or renovation need. The request may be simple or unclear.

Example:

"I need to renovate my bathroom and maybe also update the kitchen floor."

The manager starts an AI Intake session and enters the request by typing or recording voice.

### 9.2 AI-assisted intake conversation

The AI assistant asks targeted follow-up questions to collect missing project information.

The assistant may ask about:

- Customer name
- Contact person
- Phone number or email
- Project location
- Work areas
- Required renovation tasks
- Desired start date
- Expected finish date
- Payment expectations
- Materials
- Workshop needs
- Special notes or constraints

The assistant should not ask everything at once. It should focus on useful missing information.

### 9.3 Structured proposal draft

After enough information is collected, the system prepares a proposal draft.

The proposal draft can include:

- Customer details
- Project title
- Project summary
- Site or work-area list
- Scope per area
- Required trades
- Workshop needs
- Commercial notes
- Payment information
- Missing information reminders

The proposal is not final automatically. The manager reviews and edits it.

### 9.4 Proposal document

The manager can create a professional proposal document for review or customer communication.

Business value:

- Saves formatting time.
- Presents information consistently.
- Supports customer-facing communication.
- Helps avoid informal or incomplete proposal documents.

### 9.5 Proposal approval and official project creation

After review, the manager confirms the proposal and turns it into an official project/order record.

At this point, the project becomes ready for execution planning.

### 9.6 Work area and site planning

The project is divided into sites or work packages.

Examples:

- Bathroom
- Kitchen
- Hallway
- Bedroom
- Exterior wall
- Roof
- Apartment unit 1
- Apartment unit 2

Each site can have its own scope, schedule, progress, tasks, issues, materials, and workshop assignment.

### 9.7 Workshop coordination

The manager assigns workshops to project sites.

The system supports a workshop-based execution model, meaning external partners are central to how work is planned.

Business value:

- Clarifies who is responsible for each area.
- Reduces scheduling confusion.
- Makes trade coverage visible.
- Helps managers avoid conflicts.

### 9.8 Project tracking

During execution, the manager tracks project progress.

Tracked information can include:

- Progress percentage
- Tasks
- Completed work
- Open issues
- Blockers
- Materials
- Photos
- Workshop status
- Timeline updates
- Next actions

Business value:

- Creates a project history.
- Helps monitor real progress.
- Gives evidence for decisions.
- Supports better communication with customers and workshops.

### 9.9 AI monitoring

AI Monitoring reviews the structured project tracking information and explains project health.

It can help identify:

- Delays
- Blockers
- Missing information
- Incomplete tasks
- Risk areas
- Progress mismatch
- Recommended next actions

AI Monitoring does not replace manager judgment. It supports the manager by turning project data into understandable management insight.

### 9.10 Invoicing and documents

The system supports invoice-related workflows and document exports.

Business value:

- Keeps billing connected to customers and projects.
- Reduces repeated manual preparation.
- Helps maintain consistent billing documents.
- Supports a clearer finance workflow.

## 13. AI Value Explained

AI in OMRAN ERP is used as an assistant, not as an uncontrolled decision maker.

### 13.1 What AI helps with

- Understanding informal project requests.
- Asking relevant follow-up questions.
- Structuring intake information.
- Preparing proposal drafts.
- Supporting voice-based intake.
- Summarizing project health.
- Explaining risks and delays.
- Recommending practical next actions.

### 13.2 What AI should not do

AI should not:

- Invent phone numbers.
- Invent prices.
- Invent exact dates.
- Invent materials.
- Invent workshop names.
- Approve final project records by itself.
- Make official billing decisions without manager review.
- Replace construction manager judgment.

### 13.3 Why this AI approach is valuable

Construction management requires accuracy and accountability. The best AI role is to reduce repetitive work, improve completeness, and explain information clearly while keeping final control with the manager.

## 14. Manager Control and Governance

The product is designed around manager approval.

Control principles:

- AI suggestions are reviewable.
- Proposal fields can be edited.
- Missing information remains visible.
- Official records are created after confirmation.
- Project health explanations support decisions, but do not force them.
- Business-critical actions remain under manager responsibility.

This matters because construction projects involve real costs, customer commitments, legal risk, and scheduling consequences.

- Manager review is expected before proposal confirmation.

These safeguards help the product behave like a management system rather than a loose note-taking tool.

## 25. Best Practices for Managers

### 25.1 During intake

- Provide customer details as early as possible.
- Describe work areas clearly.
- Mention known dates and unknown dates separately.
- State whether information is confirmed or only estimated.
- Record workshop preferences if they already exist.

### 25.2 During proposal review

- Check customer names and contact information.
- Review every site and work scope.
- Confirm dates.
- Review payment terms.
- Check missing information flags.
- Correct AI mistakes before approval.

### 25.3 During execution

- Update progress regularly.
- Add photos for important milestones.
- Record issues immediately.
- Keep material status current.
- Review warnings frequently.
- Confirm workshop schedules early.

### 25.4 During monitoring

- Treat AI Monitoring as decision support.
- Investigate warnings before acting.
- Use monitoring summaries to prioritize follow-up.
- Keep tracking data complete so monitoring is more reliable.
